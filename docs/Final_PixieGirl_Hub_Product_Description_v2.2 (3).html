02 How the Platform Works

2.1 Multiple Brands, One System

The platform hosts two separate companies under one roof. Pixie Girl Global and Faitlyn each operate as fully independent legal entities with their own production, inventory, financial records (separate books), customer segments, document templates, and identity. Where they trade with each other it is booked as a real inter-company transaction. You log in once and switch between them with a single click, but their books never mix.

2.2 The Single Source of Truth (SSOT)

Right now, your inventory exists in multiple places. This creates a constant risk of overselling. The Hub becomes the single source of truth for all inventory. Every sale — whether it happens on your storefront, WooCommerce, Instagram DM, or in-person — deducts from one central stock count.

2.3 Your Own Storefront, Not Shopify

Instead of relying on Shopify (which charges monthly fees, limits your customisation, and keeps your customer data on their servers), the Hub includes a fully custom-built e-commerce storefront on your own domain. It is designed specifically for the wig and hair industry, supports multi-currency pricing with automatic IP-based detection, and is optimised for search engines so customers in Lagos, London, and New York can find you organically.

2.4 Omnichannel Communication

Your customers reach you through Instagram DMs, WhatsApp, email, website chat, and in-person visits. The Hub pulls all of these into a single inbox so your team never misses a message and every conversation is linked to the customer's profile, purchase history, and open orders.

2.5 Built to Scale

Whether you have five users or fifty, two sales channels or ten, the system handles it. The e-commerce storefront is built to handle traffic spikes during flash sales without slowing down.

2.6 Customer Retention Built In

Acquiring a new customer costs five to ten times more than keeping an existing one. The platform includes a dedicated Customer Retention & Loyalty module that automatically rewards repeat purchases, manages referral programmes, runs subscription services, and gives you a clear view of which customers are at risk of churning — so you can act before they leave.

03 Who Can See What

Not everyone in the business should see everything. Access control is the backbone of this platform, not an afterthought — it is enforced in five layers, and a complete role-by-module matrix follows on the next page.

  • Entity-Level Isolation: Pixie Girl Global and Faitlyn are two separate legal companies with completely separate books — not two brands of one business. Each has its own customers, inventory, invoices, ledgers, and financial statements. Staff of one entity never see the other’s data; only the CEO (who owns/controls both) sees across. Where the two companies transact, it is recorded as a real inter-company invoice in each set of books. Enforce at the data layer (row-level security on entity_id), not only the UI.
  • Module-Level Access: each role is granted only the modules it needs.
  • Action-Level Permissions: create / view / edit / delete are scoped independently (Operations approves expenses; a rep only submits).
  • Record-Level Privacy: salaries, supplier cost prices, and factory costs are restricted to the CEO and designated managers.
  • Hidden Fields: factory cost, product origin, and salary figures are invisible to roles that must not see them, even inside a module they can otherwise open.
The governing principle — cost & pay are private. Across every module, cost prices and salaries are hidden from almost everyone — even Operations, the second-most-senior role. This one rule explains most of the matrix overleaf. Every permission change is logged with date, time, and actor.

5.1 Inter-Company Trade & Separate Books

Because they are two companies, every time value crosses the boundary between them it is recorded as a real invoice/purchase pair, booked in each entity’s own ledger — never as a silent internal transfer. This is what keeps Pixie’s investor-facing books defensible. There are two such flows.

Flow 1 — Faitlyn styles Pixie’s hair

When Pixie’s hair lands in Nigeria and is dispatched to Faitlyn for styling, Faitlyn invoices Pixie for the styling service, priced per job by Faitlyn. So Faitlyn books revenue (styling sold) and Pixie books a cost (styling bought). That styling cost flows into Pixie’s per-wig landed cost in Production (6.24) carrying the Faitlyn invoice reference.

Flow 1 — styling (per job, Faitlyn-priced) PXG hair arrives Nigeria → dispatched to FLH → FLH styles → FLH issues styling invoice → PXG
  FLH books: revenue (service) · PXG books: cost into per-wig landed cost (ref FLH-INV-…)

Flow 2 — Faitlyn sells a Pixie wig

Faitlyn cannot make Pixie hair. To sell or use a Pixie wig, Faitlyn first purchases it from Pixie at a wholesale (inter-company) price — set in the Pricing module (6.25) with a minimal-margin floor, so Pixie never sells to its sister company below a fair price (protecting the investor). The unit moves from Pixie’s inventory into Faitlyn’s, where Faitlyn may resell it or use it in a service.

Flow 2 — wig transfer (wholesale, min-margin) FLH needs a Pixie wig → PXG issues wholesale sale → FLH purchase → unit moves PXG inventory → FLH inventory
  PXG books: wholesale revenue (≥ min margin) · FLH books: stock purchase at cost

Separate Books & Reporting

  • Two independent ledgers (not filtered views of one): each entity produces its own clean Income Statement / P&L, balance sheet, and tax position.
  • Every inter-company transaction is matched (FLH invoice ↔ PXG cost; PXG sale ↔ FLH purchase) and fully auditable — an investor reviewing Pixie’s books sees complete, uncontaminated accounts.
  • No investor login; accountability is served by the quality of Pixie’s books and statements, which the system must produce flawlessly.

Shared Money Rules

  • Display currencies: NGN base, auto-converted to USD, GBP, EUR, CAD, GHS by IP detection.
  • PXG storefront quantity discount: 2 items = $10 off, 3+ = $22 off; custom/styled orders excluded.
  • Payment gateways: Paystack & Nomba (local Naira), Stripe (international) — credentials CEO-only, all changes logged.
  • Cancellation timer: free within 3 hours; restocking fee thereafter; custom orders 50% fee, non-refundable.

6.2 Sales & Quotations

This module handles creating formal offers and converting them into confirmed orders. At the point of sale, your team selects whether the customer is a Walk-In (takes the product immediately) or Dispatch (needs delivery). Walk-ins close normally. Dispatch orders flow into Logistics.

Quotation Builder

Your team creates a quotation by selecting the customer, adding products with quantities and prices, applying discounts or coupon codes, and setting payment terms. The system calculates totals and taxes automatically. Quotes can be sent via WhatsApp or email with your branded layout. If loyalty points or referral rewards apply, they are computed automatically.

Installment Payments & Payment Models

Every order supports installment / partial payment — the customer can pay any amount, any time, against the order balance. The system tracks amount paid, balance due, and the full payment history on each order. Every partial is its own line in the payments ledger (sales_order_payments), so the running total is always provable. When the balance hits zero, the order auto-flips to Paid and continues down its normal fulfilment path.

What triggers fulfilment depends on the product type — each product/variant carries a payment_model field with one of two values:

  • Layaway (default for stock items): stock is reserved on order placement but no work begins until the order is paid in full. Customers can pay any amount on any cadence. If they go silent for a configurable window (default 60 days, no payment), the order auto-cancels under the standard cancellation policy (restocking fee on stock items). Zero exposure on Pixie’s side — no labour spent until cash is in.
  • Deposit-triggered (default for custom / styled items): a CEO-set minimum deposit (default 50%) unlocks fulfilment. Once the deposit clears, the order flips to In Production and styling work begins; the balance is collected before dispatch. This pairs cleanly with the existing custom-order cancellation rule (50% non-refundable) — the deposit threshold and the non-refundable share line up, so labour put into a styled wig is always covered even if the customer walks away.

The model defaults from the product type but is overridable per-product. The Pricing engine (6.25) shows the model on every product card, and the storefront PDP communicates it clearly to the customer at checkout (“Pay any amount — ships when paid in full” vs “50% deposit to start your custom wig”).

How Customers Pay Installments

Three paths are built into the system. At launch, only the gateway paths are enabled; the staff-recorded manual path stays off until Faith hires a dedicated Finance lead (see Business Setup 6.21 — toggle “Allow staff-recorded manual payments”, OFF by default).

  • Tokenised pay-link (live at launch): every order has a public, single-purpose payment URL (reuses the existing public_tracking_token on sales_orders — same token, second purpose). The system messages it via Smartcomm/WhatsApp/email with the running balance — e.g. “Hi Adaeze, you’ve paid ₦80,000 of ₦200,000 on Order PXG-1024. Pay any amount: [link].” The link routes through Paystack / Opay / Nomba / Stripe with automatic fallback (6.21).
  • Customer account self-serve (live at launch): in the storefront customer account, the My Orders view shows every order with a live balance, full payment history, and a Pay now / Pay any amount button per order. Same gateways, same fallback.
  • Staff-recorded manual payment (capability built, disabled at launch): from the Sales order detail screen, a future Finance lead will be able to record a direct bank transfer the customer made into the company account, by entering the bank-issued transaction ID (mandatory) and optionally a receipt screenshot — the same audit anchor used in Cash Request (6.32). This path is intentionally OFF until Finance is hired, because separation of duties requires that the person taking the cash isn’t the same person marking the order paid. Once the seat exists and the toggle is flipped ON, the path is live with zero code change.

Why everything goes through a gateway at launch: Paystack / Opay / Nomba / Stripe each provide independent confirmation that the money arrived. The 1.5% fee (already absorbed into the published price via Pricing 6.25) is the deliberate cost of that control. The moment Faith hires Finance, the manual path unlocks and direct-transfer customers can be served without the fee.

Gentle automated reminders are sent via Smartcomm (6.17) at configurable cadence on outstanding balances — tone is friendly, never aggressive, and the reminder schedule is editable per brand in Business Setup. Reminders pause automatically the moment a payment clears.

Why Replace Shopify?

Shopify is a powerful platform, but it comes with limitations that hold your business back:

  • Monthly Fees: Shopify charges a recurring fee plus transaction percentages on every sale. Your custom storefront eliminates these ongoing costs.
  • Limited Customisation: Shopify themes restrict how your store looks and functions. Your storefront is designed from scratch for the wig and hair industry — every feature serves your specific needs.
  • Data Ownership: On Shopify, your customer data sits on their servers. With your own storefront, all data lives in your database, fully under your control.
  • SEO Control: Shopify limits your ability to optimise for search engines. Your storefront gives you full control over URLs, meta tags, structured data, server-side rendering, and page speed.
  • Integration Depth: Instead of connecting to Shopify via APIs (with sync delays and limitations), your storefront is built directly into the Hub — inventory updates are instant, customer data is unified, and orders appear in real time.

What You Get

Everything you had on Shopify, and significantly more:

  • At clock-in and clock-out only: timestamp, GPS coordinates (browser location), and the device IP. Nothing is captured between punches — this is presence verification, not live tracking.
  • On-site / Off-site stamp: the system compares the coordinates to the brand’s registered work site(s) and stamps each punch On-site or Off-site, with the distance.
  • IP as a secondary signal: captured server-side automatically; gives rough city-level corroboration but is never relied on alone (GPS is the accurate signal).

First-Time Consent & the Permission Prompt

The first time a staff member clocks in on a device, the app shows a one-time notice explaining that location is captured at clock-in/out for attendance, and asks them to agree. The phone then shows the browser prompt — “Pixie Girl wants to know your location” — which the employee grants. This consent is also part of their employment terms, so it is transparent, not silent.

If location is denied or unavailable: the punch still records (attendance is never blocked by a GPS failure) but is flagged “location not shared” for HR to follow up. A genuinely off-site punch is flagged for review, not blocked — so a rep legitimately at a client, or someone with weak GPS, is never locked out. 6.24 Production & Landed CostBoth entities

Production owns cost and detail for every wig — from the factory in China, through shipping and customs, to a finished product ready for sale. At the end, you have the exact cost of each individual wig, which flows into that entity’s Stock (6.9) as its Cost Price. Both companies produce and run their own Production module, each ring-fenced to its own books: Pixie Girl Global manufactures Pixie hair (global), and Faitlyn manufactures everything except Pixie hair (its own China factory portion, its own inventory). The difference: Pixie does not style — it buys styling from Faitlyn as an inter-company service (see below); Faitlyn styles in-house.

Cost Captured Per Wig, Not Per Batch

Each wig is costed at variant level — style, lace type, colour, and cap size. Costs that are billed per shipment (freight, clearing, 3PL) are split fairly across the wigs they carry, so every wig carries its own true landed-and-styled cost rather than a rough average.

The Cost Build-Up

  • Factory Cost: The base cost of producing the wig, recorded in RMB and converted at the actual rate of the funding source used for that production run — so the figure reflects real money spent.
  • Forwarding & Freight: China-side consolidation, international freight, insurance, and customs/duty — pro-rated to each unit (by weight or volume where captured, otherwise per unit).
  • 3PL to Showroom: Final delivery from the port/airport to the Lagos showroom.
  • Styling: For Faitlyn, an in-house cost (labour + chemicals + packaging) via a standard recipe per style/process. For Pixie, styling is not in-house — it is bought from Faitlyn and enters Pixie’s cost as an inter-company styling invoice (priced per job by Faitlyn) carrying the FLH-INV reference.

Payment-processing fees absorbed into the published price. Formula: gross = (net + fixed_fee) ÷ (1 − pct_fee), then charm-rounded up; cap-aware so capped fees don’t over-charge. 6.32 Cash Request & DisbursementBoth entities

A dedicated workflow for any staff member to request company money before the spend happens, with explicit Finance validation, CEO approval, and Finance disbursement — producing audit-grade evidence at every step. This module sits alongside Expense Management (6.7), which covers the “I already paid out of pocket and need it back” case. Cash Request covers the “I need company money to do something” case — petty cash, fuel float, vendor deposit, travel advance, urgent operational payment.

The Workflow (User → Finance → CEO → Finance)

Every Cash Request follows the same four-stage lifecycle, with full history at each step:

  • Stage 1 — Submit (any user): the requester opens the Cash Request form, picks a type (Cash Advance for money needed before spending, or Reimbursement for an already-incurred cost), enters amount + currency, purpose, the cost-centre/account it should hit, supporting docs (vendor quote, invoice photo, justification), and submits. The system auto-fills the staffer’s bank details (account name, bank, account number) from their HR profile (6.11); if those are missing the form blocks submission and prompts the user to update their profile first — this prevents a known failure mode where the request stalls at disbursement.
  • Stage 2 — Finance validates: Finance receives the request in their queue (with a notification via Smartcomm, 6.17) and chooses one of three actions: Validate (forwards to CEO), Send Back for Revision (returns to the requester with a required note — the requester edits and resubmits, history preserved), or Reject (terminal, with a required reason).
  • Stage 3 — CEO approves: the CEO receives the validated request and chooses Approve, Send Back for Revision, or Reject. Approval returns the request to Finance for disbursement.
  • Stage 4 — Finance disburses (manual bank transfer): Finance makes the actual transfer manually from the business bank account — not through a payment gateway — to avoid paying processor fees on internal/staff outflows. Once the transfer is done, Finance enters the bank-issued transaction ID (mandatory) and optionally uploads a receipt screenshot. The request moves to status Disbursed and is automatically posted as an Expense (6.7) under the cost-centre/account the requester chose.
Why manual disbursement (not Paystack). Paystack, Opay, Nomba and Stripe exist to receive money from customers — the 1.5% fee is the cost of that receipt. For internal outflows (paying a staffer their advance), the same gateway would simply charge a fee to move company money from one bank account to another. Finance disbursing manually via the business banking app is free, instant, and produces a transaction ID that maps cleanly to the bank statement. The system requires that ID as the audit anchor.

Transaction ID, Reconciliation & Audit Trail

The transaction ID is the audit linchpin of this module. It is mandatory at disbursement and feeds directly into Bank Reconciliation (6.6): the disbursement appears as an unmatched outflow waiting for the corresponding bank-statement line, and a one-click match (or auto-match if the bank export carries the same reference) closes the loop. Format is intentionally free text — different Nigerian banks (Zenith, GTB, UBA, Kuda, etc.) use different reference formats, so the system requires something but doesn’t try to validate the shape; reconciliation against the bank statement is what proves correctness. A match-status pill (Pending Match / Matched / Manual Match) is visible on every disbursed request, and unmatched disbursements older than a configurable window surface as an AI Insights alert.

Future-proofing the embeddings (important)

pgvector columns have a fixed dimension (vector(1536) for the chosen model). Switching to a different embedding model later means re-embedding everything — so the schema is designed from day one to make that a planned migration, not a rebuild:

  • Store the source text with every vector — never only the vector — so anything can be re-embedded at any time.
  • Record embedding_model + embedding_version on each row, so two models can run side-by-side during a migration and stale vectors are identifiable.
  • Keep embeddings in a dedicated table keyed to source records (not a column on business tables), so a model change never touches the core schema.
  • Abstract the embedding call behind one service in Node, so swapping provider/model is a one-place change.
Net effect: a future move off vector(1536) becomes “add new model column → re-embed from retained source text → switch retrieval → drop old” — a controlled migration, with no data loss and no core-schema disruption.

8.2 Agent Architecture (Deep Dive)

Praxis is the single conversational face; underneath sits a multi-agent system coordinated by an Orchestrator. This section is the technical contract for the engineers building it.

The agents

  • Orchestrator: receives the user message (text, or Whisper transcript), classifies intent, decomposes into ordered steps, routes each to a specialist agent, manages state across steps, and assembles the final reply. It owns the conversation loop and the confirmation gate.
  • Action Agent: matches an intent to one or more Action-Catalogue entries, fills the payload (asking the user for missing fields), and — only after confirmation — calls the real backend endpoint with the user’s own auth/permissions.
  • Query Agent: answers read-only questions about the business’s data. Translates a question into a parameterised, permission-scoped query (never free-form SQL from the model), runs it, and summarises results.
  • Insights Agent: the tier-2 narration pass (6.30) — reads summarised rule-engine output and writes briefings/reports.
  • Drafting Agent: generates text (invoice notes, replies, campaign copy) — no system writes.

The request lifecycle (single & multi-step)

Orchestrator loop 1 Input (text / Whisper transcript) → 2 Intent classify → 3 RAG retrieve (catalogue + context, permission-scoped) →
4 Plan (decompose into steps; resolve order & dependencies) → 5 per step: match action → fill payload (ask for missing fields) →
6 Confirm (show exact action + payload; await explicit Yes) → 7 Execute (call endpoint w/ user permissions) → 8 Verify + log → next step
9 Assemble reply + result links. Any step can halt & ask; low confidence → ask, never guess.

A multi-step request (“sell 10 to Faitlyn, create the client first”) becomes a plan of dependent steps. If step 2 needs the client id created in step 1, the orchestrator sequences them and carries the result forward. Each write step has its own confirmation; reads flow freely.

Confidence & disambiguation: intent matching returns a confidence score. Above threshold → propose the action and confirm. Below → present the top candidates and ask. No match in the catalogue → state plainly that the action doesn’t exist and suggest requesting it from JBS Praxis. The model never invents an endpoint.

8.3 The Action Catalogue (Grounding)

The catalogue is the single mechanism that stops hallucination: Praxis can only ever choose from registered, real, permitted actions. It is a curated registry generated from the backend’s OpenAPI specification — not from runtime events — so it stays in sync with the actual API.

Schema (per action)

Field Meaning
action_id Stable identifier (e.g. invoice.create).
title / description Plain-language name + what it does (used for intent matching & the confirm card).
method / route The real HTTP verb + endpoint (from OpenAPI).
payload_schema JSON schema of inputs (types, required, enums) — drives payload-fill & validation.
required_permission The exact RBAC grant needed (from 6.27). Checked before execution.
entity_scope PXG / FLH / both — enforces entity isolation.
is_write If true, mandatory confirmation before execution.

examples Sample utterances & payloads — improve matching, embedded for retrieval.

How it is kept honest

  • Generated, then curated: a build step reads the OpenAPI spec and upserts catalogue rows; a human sets ai_enabled, descriptions, and examples. New endpoints appear as disabled until reviewed — nothing is auto-exposed.
  • Permission & entity checks at call time: even if matched, an action runs only if the current user holds required_permission and the entity_scope matches the active entity. The AI never exceeds the user’s own access.
  • No free-form calls: Praxis cannot construct an endpoint that isn’t in the catalogue. “No action” is a first-class, designed response.
  • Embedded for retrieval: titles, descriptions, and examples are embedded into pgvector so the Action Agent retrieves the right few candidates per request instead of scanning everything.
Why this is the backbone: grounding the agent in a permission-aware registry of real endpoints is what makes “let it do everything” safe. Capability grows by enabling catalogue entries — a governance action, not a code change.

8.4 The RAG Pipeline (Retrieval)

RAG keeps Praxis affordable and accurate: instead of sending the model bulk data, it retrieves only the relevant few snippets. The pipeline is local (pgvector) with API-based embeddings.

What gets embedded

  • The Action Catalogue (titles, descriptions, examples) — for intent → action matching.
  • Reference knowledge: the Product Description, SOPs, policies, entity facts — so Praxis answers “how do we…” correctly.
  • Selected business records as needed (e.g. product names for fuzzy matching) — embedded with strict scope tags.

Ingestion & retrieval flow

Pipeline Ingest: source text → chunk (sized, overlapped) → embed via OpenAI API → store in embeddings (vector + source_text + model + version + scope tags)
Retrieve: user query → embed → permission/entity-scoped similarity search in pgvector → top-k chunks → into the prompt → model

Security in retrieval (critical)

  • Permission-scoped retrieval: every embedding row carries the access scope of its source. Retrieval filters by the current user’s permissions and active entity before similarity ranking — so Praxis can never surface content the user couldn’t see in the UI (e.g. cost prices, salaries, the other entity’s data).
  • Entity isolation: retrieval is filtered by entity_id; PXG context never leaks into an FLH conversation and vice-versa.
  • No raw dumps: the model receives small, relevant chunks — never whole tables — which also caps token cost.

Cost levers (tuned in 6.31)

  • Top-k size, chunk size, and context budget are configurable; smaller = cheaper.
  • Frequent retrievals (e.g. the catalogue) are cached; embeddings are computed once at ingest, not per query.
  • The Insights pass reads pre-computed summaries, not raw rows.

8.5 Guardrails, Safety & Governance

Because Praxis can act on the business, safety is engineered, not assumed. These rules are mandatory across the AI layer.

Action safety

  • Confirmation before every write: any data-changing action shows the exact action and a payload summary and requires explicit user confirmation. No exceptions in V1.
  • Permission inheritance: Praxis acts as the user — it can never do anything the user couldn’t do directly. Every call is permission- and entity-checked server-side.
  • Catalogue-only: no action outside the registry; “no action exists” is the designed fallback.
  • Reversibility & audit: every AI action is written to the central audit_log (actor = user via Praxis, before/after, timestamp), so any change is traceable and reviewable.

Data & privacy

  • Permission-scoped retrieval (8.4) prevents the model from ever seeing restricted fields or the other entity’s data.
  • No training on their data: business data is used only for in-context retrieval at request time; it is not used to train external models.
  • PII handling: customer/staff PII is minimised in prompts; sensitive fields (salaries, cost prices, origin) are never embedded into general-access vectors.
  • Vendor keys: encrypted, CEO-controlled, never in the frontend (8.1).

Prompt-injection & abuse defence

  • Retrieved content and user text are treated as data, not instructions; the orchestrator’s control logic decides actions, the model proposes — it cannot self-authorise a write.
  • Tool calls are validated against the payload schema before execution; malformed or out-of-scope calls are rejected.
  • Rate limits and the usage cap (6.31) bound both cost and abuse.

Access & rollout

V1: Praxis is CEO-only. Expansion to other roles is a deliberate, budgeted decision in AI Control (6.31); when granted, each user still operates strictly within their own RBAC permissions and entity scope.

Honest boundary: Praxis configures and acts within the system as built. It does not invent new module behaviour or bypass controls; genuinely new capabilities are a JBS Praxis build. This is stated to the user, not hidden.
